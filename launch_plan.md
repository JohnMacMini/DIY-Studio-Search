# DIY Studio Search — Launch Plan

**Project:** DIY Studio Search — a directory of DIY studios and classes across North Carolina
**Operator:** John, solo builder, part-time
**Current state:** 55 pottery studios listed statewide; leatherworking next in queue
**Monetization model:** Featured listings — studios pay for top placement, richer cards, and attribution data

---

## Phase 1 — Pre-Launch (Days 0–30)

The goal here is simple: make the directory ready to show the world without embarrassment. That means filling it out, making it findable, and getting honest feedback before you open the doors.

Start by adding leatherworking studios to the directory. This gives you a second craft category and signals to visitors (and Google) that the site is a real resource, not a one-trick showcase. As you add listings, wire up the studio submission form using a Google Form — keep it simple, just name, location, craft type, website, and a contact email. The form doesn't need to be beautiful; it needs to exist so studios can self-serve if they find you organically.

On the technical side, get basic SEO meta tags in place: page titles, descriptions, and Open Graph tags so links look good when shared on social. Submit your sitemap to Google Search Console and request indexing. None of this is glamorous work, but skipping it means you're building a house no one can find.

Once the directory feels solid, do a soft launch to friends, family, and any crafters you personally know. Ask them to actually use it — search for a pottery studio near them, try submitting a fake listing, click around on mobile. The feedback you get from people who genuinely want to help you succeed is worth far more at this stage than any analytics dashboard.

---

## Phase 2 — Growth (Days 30–90)

The prime directive for this phase: **list first, outreach second.** Never reach out to a studio that isn't already in your directory. The credibility of "you're already on the map" is infinitely higher than "we'd love to add you someday." Every studio you plan to contact should be findable on DIY Studio Search before you send a single email.

Spend the first weeks of this phase building out listing density. More studios, more craft types, more cities. Free listings cost studios nothing and cost you only time. Your goal is to make the directory genuinely useful to someone who doesn't know it exists yet — the kind of thing a potter finds via Google and immediately bookmarks.

In parallel, stand up an Instagram presence. The craft audience lives there. You don't need a full content strategy; you need a consistent habit. Post a studio spotlight a few times a week — a photo, a one-liner about what makes them worth visiting, and a link to their listing. Tag the studio. Some of them will repost it, which is free reach into their follower base. Over 60 days this compounds quietly.

Start reaching out to studio owners around week five or six, once you have enough listings to look authoritative. The goal in this phase is not paid conversions — it's relationships and testimonials. Ask studio owners what they'd want to see in a listing. Ask if they'd be willing to say something kind about the directory. A handful of genuine quotes from real studio owners gives you social proof that matters when you launch paid tiers.

---

## Phase 3 — Monetization Launch (Day 90+)

Don't introduce paid listings until you can walk into a pitch with real data. That means waiting until studios have had at least a few weeks of click activity from their free listing. The number doesn't have to be huge — even 40 profile views in a month is a real, attributable number that most studio owners have never seen from any marketing channel.

The featured listing tier should launch with exactly one slot per city per craft category. Scarcity is a feature, not a limitation. "There are three pottery studios in Asheville, and only one of them can be Featured" is a more compelling pitch than any feature list.

Pricing: $25/month standard, $20/month for early adopters who commit in the first 90 days. These numbers are low enough that a studio owner doesn't need to run it up the chain, but high enough to cover your time and signal that this is a real product.

What makes featured worth paying for isn't just placement — it's the full package: top position in city/craft results, a richer listing card with a photo, a pull quote from the owner, a Book a Class button, a monthly click report delivered to their inbox, and a Featured badge they can share on their own social channels. That badge matters. It's external validation they can put on their own Instagram stories.

Your banking background is a genuine differentiator in these conversations. Most small studio owners have never had anyone frame their marketing spend in terms of cost-per-acquisition, attribution, or ROI. When you show a pottery studio owner that their $20/month featured listing drove 80 clicks to their class booking page last month, and ask them what their average class booking is worth, that math does your selling for you. You're not a directory salesperson — you're a guy who understands financial return on ad spend, talking to someone who's probably never heard that framing applied to their kiln studio in Carrboro.

---

## Phase 4 — Scale (Months 6–12)

Once the monetization model is proven with pottery and leatherworking, the expansion path is straightforward: more crafts (woodworking, glassblowing, jewelry making, fiber arts, ceramics, bookbinding), more NC cities, then eventually other states. Each new craft category is a new audience slice with its own studio community and its own Instagram hashtag ecosystem to tap.

The next meaningful upsell is individual studio profile pages — a dedicated URL for each studio with their full class schedule, photos, owner bio, and reviews. Studios already paying for featured listings are your first audience for this. It's a natural next step, not a cold pitch.

The highest-ceiling opportunity in the longer term is partnership with NC arts councils, tourism boards, and community colleges that offer craft workshops. These organizations have audiences, credibility, and sometimes budget. A white-label version of DIY Studio Search — or a co-branded integration — could expand your distribution significantly without requiring you to build a bigger directory from scratch.

The operating principle throughout all of this: build the free layer generously, prove value with data, and let the conversion be obvious rather than pushed.
